Responsible development mentality

  • Compassion
  • Humility

Compassion

For the developers of the past whose code you inherited

If you inherited legacy code that is buggy and unreadable, put yourself in the shoes of the software developers who came before you and take pity.

  • You don't know how bad the time and budget constraints were.
  • You don't know how bad the client was to work with.
  • You may not know what parts were in-progress before the project was abandoned.

If you have mercy on past developers of the past, then other developers may have mercy on you when your project goes sideways.

For the developers of the future who will inherit your code

Every line of code you write costs the time and energy of future software developers who have to work with it. You can do a disproportionately large amount of damage in a short time by writing hundreds of lines of smelly code that others will have to sniff-out the value of.

Therefore, you shouldn't consider your code clean until someone with no context can understand it.

If you have mercy on the future inheritors of your code, then maybe the code you inherit will also be clean.

For the users

Specifications and requirements will not always come in a perfect form. Often, the users don't know what they want, and may not like what they see when it's given to them.

Understanding their work and being open to their feedback will result in a superior product.

Humility

Pride prevents you from seeing issues in your code.

Humble developers have learned no code is not perfect, and rely on rigorous testing and the assistance of their peers to ensure a quality product.
